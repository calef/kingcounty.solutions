  Black History Month is not just about celebrating the Black voices and leaders of the past who have shaped our world through resilience, innovation, and leadership. Historical figures like Martin Luther King Jr., Malcolm X, and Rosa Parks fought for civil rights and justice, while pioneers like

  Madame C.J. Walker transformed Black hair care and Marie Van Brittan Brown created the first home security system.

  Black History Month is also about honoring the voices and leaders of today and tomorrow—those who uplift their communities, create opportunities, and inspire the next generation. That spirit of leadership lives on through individuals like Bonita Armstrong and Kendrick Davis, who help shape the future at Boys & Girls Clubs of King County through strength, unity, and compassion.

  **Bonita Armstrong’s** journey with the Clubs began in 2010 as a Community Advisory Board (CAB) member at the Smilow Rainier Vista Club, where she dedicated herself to ensuring youth had access to the support and resources they needed to thrive. Her commitment and leadership led her to her current role as Chair of the Board of Directors, where she now helps guide Boys & Girls Clubs of King County to ensure that every young person, regardless of race or background, has the chance to succeed.

  _“We want to help you be the best version of yourself—whatever that looks like_,” Bonita says. _“Success is not one-size-fits-all. Some [youth] will go to college, some will take a different path, but all are capable of greatness.”_

  Just as Bonita’s leadership ensures a strong foundation for the Clubs, **Kendrick Davis’** work helps bring that vision to life in the day-to-day experiences of young people.

  Kendrick knows firsthand the power of the Clubs. As an alum of the North Seattle Club, he found mentorship, guidance, and a sense of belonging that helped shape his future. During his professional career, he returned to the Clubs where he is now an Area Director overseeing Rotary, Lowell, and Rainer Vista Clubs; Kendrick is ensuring that the next generation of youth—especially Black youth—see leaders who look like them and understand their experiences.

  _“You belong in every room you step into. Your voice matters. Take up space, ask questions, and never doubt your worth,”_ Kendrick encourages.

  Their work is a testament to the power of representation and mentorship. This is a reminder that the fight for inclusion, opportunity, and equity is ongoing, and it starts with empowering today’s youth to lead tomorrow’s change.

  Black History Month is not just about remembering—it is about continuing to build. It is about ensuring that every young person of every race has the tools, support, and confidence to make their mark on the world. At Boys & Girls Clubs of King County, that work is happening every day.

Black History Month celebrates not only historical figures who fought for civil rights but also contemporary leaders who uplift communities and inspire future generations. Bonita Armstrong and Kendrick Davis exemplify this spirit at Boys & Girls Clubs of King County. 

Armstrong began her journey in 2010 as a Community Advisory Board member and now serves as chair of the Board of Directors, advocating for youth access to resources and opportunities. She emphasizes that success varies for each individual, encouraging youth to be their best selves. 

Davis, an alumnus of the North Seattle Club, returned as an area director to ensure that young people, particularly Black youth, see leaders who resonate with their experiences. He inspires them to recognize their value and take up space in various environments. 

Their leadership highlights the importance of representation and mentorship in the ongoing fight for inclusion and equity. Black History Month serves as a reminder to continue building a supportive framework for all youth, equipping them with the tools and confidence necessary to make significant contributions to society. At Boys & Girls Clubs of King County, this mission is actively pursued every day.
