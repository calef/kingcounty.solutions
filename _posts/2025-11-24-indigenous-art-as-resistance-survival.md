  <h5 class="tmc-small"><span style="color: #4d5909;"><b>Cannupa Hanska Luger (Mandan, Hidatsa, Arikara, and Lakota)</b> </span></h5>
  <p><span data-contrast="auto"><a href="https://www.cannupahanska.com/">Cannupa Hanska Luger</a>’s work centers community, ceremony, and collective action. One of his most powerful projects, </span><a href="https://www.cannupahanska.com/social-engagement/every-one"><b><i><span data-contrast="auto">Every One</span></i></b></a><span data-contrast="auto">, responds to the crisis of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women, Girls, Queer, and Two-Spirit relatives (MMIWG2S+). Community members created over 4,000 handmade clay beads, which Luger assembled into a monumental portrait mural as an act of shared witness and grief transformed into solidarity.</span><span data-ccp-props="{}"> </span></p>

  <h5 class="tmc-small"><span style="color: #4d5909;"><b>Jordan Ann Craig (Northern Cheyenne</b><b>)</b> </span></h5>
  <p><span data-contrast="auto"><a href="https://www.jordananncraig.com/">Jordan Ann Craig</a>&#8216;s art draws on Northern Cheyenne patterns, star knowledge, and stories held in family memory. Her paintings explore geometric repetition as prayer, grounding, and identity. She also teaches printmaking to Native youth, reinforcing that art is a collective inheritance.</span><span data-ccp-props="{}"> </span></p>
  <p><span data-contrast="auto">In 2025 Craig debuted a new exhibit at Northwestern University&#8217;s The Block Museum, titled </span><a href="https://www.blockmuseum.northwestern.edu/exhibitions/2025/jordan-ann-craig.html"><b><i><span data-contrast="auto">it takes a long time to stay here</span></i></b></a><span data-contrast="auto">. This exhibit features seven paintings that represent her exploration of traditional Indigenous art forms like beadwork, pottery, and textiles. </span><span data-ccp-props="{}"> </span></p>

Indigenous art serves as a vital means of survival and cultural preservation for Indigenous communities across the U.S. and beyond, acting as a tool for resistance against erasure and colonial narratives. Featured artists like Cannupa Hanska Luger, Tania Willard, Dyani White Hawk, and Jordan Ann Craig exemplify this role through their innovative works. Luger’s project, Every One, addresses the crisis of Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women, Girls, Queer, and Two-Spirit relatives, using community-created clay beads to form a mural symbolizing collective grief and solidarity. Willard’s BUSH Gallery operates outside traditional spaces, emphasizing contemporary Indigenous presence and reframing land as a gallery. White Hawk’s textile-inspired art challenges mainstream perceptions of Indigenous aesthetics, asserting their equal footing with Western abstraction. Craig’s paintings draw on Northern Cheyenne heritage, exploring themes of identity and community memory. Collectively, these artists highlight the importance of recognizing Indigenous art as a form of resistance, affirming that Indigenous communities have always existed and continue to thrive creatively and culturally. Art not only commemorates history but also shapes the future, reinforcing the need to support Indigenous artists in their courageous endeavors.
