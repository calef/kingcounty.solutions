This new order strengthens Washington State’s commitment to working directly and respectfully with sovereign Tribal Nations and sets higher expectations for how state agencies build and maintain those relationships. The points summarized here are just a few highlights and do not represent the entirety of the Order&#8217;s content.</p>
  <p>For many years, Tribal Nations have asked the state to engage more consistently, communicate earlier, and follow clearer standards when making decisions that affect Tribal people, lands, and resources. Executive Order 25-10 aims to respond to those concerns by establishing a stronger foundation for government-to-government partnerships. Under the order, state agencies will be expected to visit Tribal communities more regularly, attend events, and build relationships in person rather than only during times of conflict or urgency. The order requires agencies to consult with Tribes early in their decision-making processes whenever an action may impact a Tribal Nation. This means listening to Tribal leaders, responding to concerns, and only moving forward after sharing meaningful dialogue.</p>
  <p>A significant part of the order focuses on the recognition of Indigenous Knowledge. When agencies rely on scientific information to guide their work, they must also include Indigenous Knowledge in their research, interpretations, and recommendations whenever the project provides an appropriate opportunity to do so.</p>
  <p>The order calls for stronger support of Native art and culture across state programs and public spaces. It directs agencies to actively look for ways to feature and uplift Washington State Native artists. This includes building relationships and developing programs with Tribal artists who are enrolled in federally recognized Tribes in Washington, as well as artists whose traditional homelands extend into the state. Through these efforts, the order aims to ensure that Native art and cultural expression are visible, respected, and meaningfully included in the work of state government.</p>
  <p>To support transparency follow-through, the order strengthens how state agencies track and report their work with Tribal Nations. Each agency will prepare an annual summary of its government-to-government activities, and Tribal liaisons will now report directly to agency leadership, ensuring that Tribal matters receive appropriate attention. The Governor’s Office of Indian Affairs will develop expanded Tribal relations training for agency heads, leadership teams, and state employees so that understanding of Tribal sovereignty, history, and responsibilities is strengthened across state government.</p>
  <p>Statewide consultation guidelines will be finalized in time for the 2026 Centennial Accord, and all agencies will be required to fully implement those guidelines within one year of their release. The state will also work with Tribal Nations to improve contracting processes by identifying current barriers and creating clearer, more efficient pathways by 2026.</p>
  <p>Finally, the order requires agencies to establish protections for sensitive Tribal information and Indigenous Knowledge shared during government-to-government interactions, ensuring that confidential material is handled appropriately and respectfully. This commitment is significant, as it recognizes that such knowledge is not simply data, but part of a living cultural heritage that must be safeguarded with care. By putting these protections in place, the state acknowledges its responsibility to honor Tribal trust and uphold the integrity of the relationships it seeks to strengthen.</p>
  <p>Executive Order 25-10 marks an important step toward more respectful partnerships between the State of Washington and Tribal Nations. For the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe, these commitments affirm that any state decisions affecting our people, lands, culture, and resources must include us from the beginning rather than after plans are already in motion.</p>
  <p>The Tribal Council will continue monitoring the rollout of these changes and will keep members informed as statewide guidelines, trainings, and reporting systems take shape.</p>

  Chairman Robert de los Angeles, Vice Chairman Jim Sweet, Treasurer Joshua Gabel, and Councilmembers Jolene Williams, Richard Sikkenga Jr., and Steve De Los Angeles represented the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe at a recent session where Governor Bob Ferguson signed [Executive Order 25-10](https://governor.wa.gov/sites/default/files/exe_order/25-10%20-%20Tribal%20Relations%20%28tmp%29_1.pdf). This new order strengthens Washington State’s commitment to working directly and respectfully with sovereign Tribal Nations and sets higher expectations for how state agencies build and maintain those relationships. The points summarized here are just a few highlights and do not represent the entirety of the Order’s content.

  For many years, Tribal Nations have asked the state to engage more consistently, communicate earlier, and follow clearer standards when making decisions that affect Tribal people, lands, and resources. Executive Order 25-10 aims to respond to those concerns by establishing a stronger foundation for government-to-government partnerships. Under the order, state agencies will be expected to visit Tribal communities more regularly, attend events, and build relationships in person rather than only during times of conflict or urgency. The order requires agencies to consult with Tribes early in their decision-making processes whenever an action may impact a Tribal Nation. This means listening to Tribal leaders, responding to concerns, and only moving forward after sharing meaningful dialogue.

  A significant part of the order focuses on the recognition of Indigenous Knowledge. When agencies rely on scientific information to guide their work, they must also include Indigenous Knowledge in their research, interpretations, and recommendations whenever the project provides an appropriate opportunity to do so.

  The order calls for stronger support of Native art and culture across state programs and public spaces. It directs agencies to actively look for ways to feature and uplift Washington State Native artists. This includes building relationships and developing programs with Tribal artists who are enrolled in federally recognized Tribes in Washington, as well as artists whose traditional homelands extend into the state. Through these efforts, the order aims to ensure that Native art and cultural expression are visible, respected, and meaningfully included in the work of state government.

  To support transparency follow-through, the order strengthens how state agencies track and report their work with Tribal Nations. Each agency will prepare an annual summary of its government-to-government activities, and Tribal liaisons will now report directly to agency leadership, ensuring that Tribal matters receive appropriate attention. The Governor’s Office of Indian Affairs will develop expanded Tribal relations training for agency heads, leadership teams, and state employees so that understanding of Tribal sovereignty, history, and responsibilities is strengthened across state government.

  Statewide consultation guidelines will be finalized in time for the 2026 Centennial Accord, and all agencies will be required to fully implement those guidelines within one year of their release. The state will also work with Tribal Nations to improve contracting processes by identifying current barriers and creating clearer, more efficient pathways by 2026.

  Finally, the order requires agencies to establish protections for sensitive Tribal information and Indigenous Knowledge shared during government-to-government interactions, ensuring that confidential material is handled appropriately and respectfully. This commitment is significant, as it recognizes that such knowledge is not simply data, but part of a living cultural heritage that must be safeguarded with care. By putting these protections in place, the state acknowledges its responsibility to honor Tribal trust and uphold the integrity of the relationships it seeks to strengthen.

  Executive Order 25-10 marks an important step toward more respectful partnerships between the State of Washington and Tribal Nations. For the Snoqualmie Indian Tribe, these commitments affirm that any state decisions affecting our people, lands, culture, and resources must include us from the beginning rather than after plans are already in motion.

  The Tribal Council will continue monitoring the rollout of these changes and will keep members informed as statewide guidelines, trainings, and reporting systems take shape.

Washington State has reinforced its commitment to collaborate with sovereign tribal nations by signing a new agreement with the Snoqualmie Tribe. This agreement emphasizes the state's recognition of tribal sovereignty and the importance of government-to-government relationships. It aims to enhance cooperation on various issues, including economic development, environmental protection, and cultural preservation. Tribal leaders and state officials expressed their dedication to working together to address challenges faced by tribal communities. The agreement also includes provisions for ongoing dialogue and partnership, ensuring that tribal voices are heard in state decision-making processes. This initiative marks a significant step in fostering mutual respect and collaboration between the state and tribal nations, acknowledging the unique status of tribes and their contributions to Washington's culture and economy.
