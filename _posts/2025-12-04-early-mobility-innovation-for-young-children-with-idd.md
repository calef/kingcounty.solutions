  style=\"text-align: center;\">Early Mobility Innovation for Young Children with
  IDD</h2>\n<p>CREATE PhD student <a href=\"https://miahoffmannd.github.io/\">Mia
  Hoffman</a> and CREATE postdoctoral scholar <a href=\"https://www.ohsu.edu/people/bethany-m-sloane-pt-dpt-phd-pcs\">Dr.
  Bethany Sloane</a> are advancing new approaches to early mobility for young children
  with developmental and motor disabilities. Their work, developed through <a href=\"https://create.uw.edu/\">UW
  CREATE</a> in partnership with the Neuromechanics and Mobility Lab, shows how engineering
  and pediatric therapy can come together to support early participation and exploration
  for children with IDD.</p>\n<p><img loading=\"lazy\" decoding=\"async\" id=\"longdesc-return-16904\"
  class=\"alignright wp-image-16904\" tabindex=\"-1\" src=\"https://ihdd.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/reimagining-mobility-aids.jpg\"
  alt=\"Reimagining Mobility Aids - Mia Hoffman and Dr. Bethany Sloane\" width=\"500\"
  height=\"428\" longdesc=\"https://ihdd.org?longdesc=16904&amp;referrer=16901\" srcset=\"https://ihdd.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/reimagining-mobility-aids.jpg
  700w, https://ihdd.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/reimagining-mobility-aids-300x257.jpg
  300w, https://ihdd.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/reimagining-mobility-aids-600x513.jpg
  600w\" sizes=\"auto, (max-width: 500px) 100vw, 500px\" />At the APTA Pediatrics
  2025 Conference, Hoffman and Sloane presented a session on reimagining mobility
  aids through interdisciplinary design. Their examples included 3D printed joystick
  adaptations, sensor integrated systems that help track motor engagement, and community
  loaner programs that expand access to powered mobility in early childhood. These
  approaches were designed to support early communication, play, and interaction for
  young children with cerebral palsy and other developmental delays.</p>\n<p>A full
  recap of their conference session is available in the lab’s news post: <a href=\"https://steelelab.me.uw.edu/2025/11/apta-pediatrics-2025/\">Reimagining
  Mobility Aids &#8211; APTA Pediatrics 2025</a></p>\n<p>Dr. Sloane is also leading
  a new NIH K23 funded project focused on implementing powered mobility training within
  home based early intervention. Her research includes developing training for therapists
  and caregivers using the Permobil Explorer Mini, collecting data on how mobility
  devices are used in daily routines, and identifying barriers to early mobility for
  children under age three with significant motor delays.</p>\n<p>More about her NIH
  funded work is available here: <a href=\"https://create.uw.edu/life-changing-research-for-children-with-cerebral-palsy-gets-life-changing-funding/\">NIH
  funded mobility research for children with motor delays</a></p>\n<p>This work is
  supported by CREATE faculty advisors <a href=\"https://create.uw.edu/people-directors-steele/\">Dr.
  Katherine M. Steele</a> and <a href=\"https://create.uw.edu/people-directors-feldner/\">Dr.
  Heather Feldner</a>, whose research examines mobility, participation, and early
  developmental outcomes for children with disabilities. Together, these interdisciplinary
  efforts highlight the critical role of early mobility in supporting communication,
  social engagement, and participation for young children with IDD.</p>\n\r\n\t<div

  for Young Children with IDD\n\nCREATE PhD student [Mia Hoffman](https://miahoffmannd.github.io/)
  and CREATE postdoctoral scholar [Dr. Bethany Sloane](https://www.ohsu.edu/people/bethany-m-sloane-pt-dpt-phd-pcs)
  are advancing new approaches to early mobility for young children with developmental
  and motor disabilities. Their work, developed through [UW CREATE](https://create.uw.edu/)
  in partnership with the Neuromechanics and Mobility Lab, shows how engineering and
  pediatric therapy can come together to support early participation and exploration
  for children with IDD.\n\n![Reimagining Mobility Aids - Mia Hoffman and Dr. Bethany
  Sloane](https://ihdd.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/reimagining-mobility-aids.jpg)At
  the APTA Pediatrics 2025 Conference, Hoffman and Sloane presented a session on reimagining
  mobility aids through interdisciplinary design. Their examples included 3D printed
  joystick adaptations, sensor integrated systems that help track motor engagement,
  and community loaner programs that expand access to powered mobility in early childhood.
  These approaches were designed to support early communication, play, and interaction
  for young children with cerebral palsy and other developmental delays.\n\nA full
  recap of their conference session is available in the lab’s news post: [Reimagining
  Mobility Aids – APTA Pediatrics 2025](https://steelelab.me.uw.edu/2025/11/apta-pediatrics-2025/)\n\nDr.
  Sloane is also leading a new NIH K23 funded project focused on implementing powered
  mobility training within home based early intervention. Her research includes developing
  training for therapists and caregivers using the Permobil Explorer Mini, collecting
  data on how mobility devices are used in daily routines, and identifying barriers
  to early mobility for children under age three with significant motor delays.\n\nMore
  about her NIH funded work is available here: [NIH funded mobility research for children
  with motor delays](https://create.uw.edu/life-changing-research-for-children-with-cerebral-palsy-gets-life-changing-funding/)\n\nThis
  work is supported by CREATE faculty advisors [Dr. Katherine M. Steele](https://create.uw.edu/people-directors-steele/)
  and [Dr. Heather Feldner](https://create.uw.edu/people-directors-feldner/), whose
  research examines mobility, participation, and early developmental outcomes for
  children with disabilities. Together, these interdisciplinary efforts highlight
  the critical role of early mobility in supporting communication, social engagement,

A new initiative focuses on early mobility innovation for young children with intellectual and developmental disabilities (IDD). This program aims to improve the mobility options available to these children, recognizing that mobility is crucial for their development and independence. By introducing cutting-edge mobility devices and solutions, the initiative seeks to enhance the quality of life for children with IDD and empower them to engage more fully in their environments. 

Key components of the program include collaboration between experts in pediatric therapy, engineering, and parental input to ensure that the solutions meet the specific needs of children with IDD. The initiative also emphasizes the importance of inclusive design, making sure that mobility solutions are accessible and effective for a diverse range of abilities. 

Early intervention is highlighted as a critical factor, as timely access to mobility resources can significantly impact a child's development trajectory. Ultimately, the program aspires to foster a greater sense of independence and participation for young children with IDD, paving the way for a more inclusive society.
